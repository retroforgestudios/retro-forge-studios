Quick test: Google your business name right now. Then check Bing Places, Apple
Maps, Yelp, and Facebook. Does your phone number match on all five? Your
address? Even your hours?

For a lot of small business owners in Nebraska and Iowa, the answer is no —
and they have no idea. Maybe you moved suites three years ago and never
updated Yelp. Maybe an old landline is still listed on a directory you forgot
existed. Maybe your business name shows up as "Smith Plumbing LLC" in one
place and "Smith Plumbing Omaha" in another.

To you, that feels like a minor clerical thing. To Google, it's a red flag.

## Why this actually matters

Search engines cross-reference your business information — name, address,
phone number (marketers call this "NAP") — across every directory and
platform they can find. When those details match everywhere, it tells Google
your business is legitimate and well-maintained, which helps your local
rankings. When they don't match, Google gets less confident about which
version is correct, and that uncertainty can quietly knock you down the map
pack results — right where your competitors are waiting.

It's not just an algorithm problem, either. A customer who calls the old
number, drives to the old address, or shows up during hours that are actually
wrong just has a bad experience before they've spent a dollar with you. Local
intent searches aren't small either — Google reports that 46% of all searches
have local intent, meaning nearly half the people searching are looking for a
business near them, right now. If your listing sends them the wrong
information, that's a lost customer, not just a lost click.

## How to fix it (in about an hour)

**1. Make a master record.**
Write down the exact, correct version of your business name, address, phone
number, website, and hours. This is your source of truth — every listing
should match it exactly, including how you abbreviate "Street" or format your
phone number.

**2. Audit the big four first.**
Check Google Business Profile, Facebook, Apple Maps, and Bing Places. These
are the platforms most likely to show up when someone searches your business
name or "[your service] near me."

**3. Check the directories you forgot about.**
Yelp, Yellow Pages, Nextdoor, and industry-specific directories (think Houzz
for contractors, Avvo for attorneys, Healthgrades for medical practices) often
have listings that were auto-generated years ago and never claimed. Search
your business name plus "directory" or "listing" to surface ones you didn't
create yourself.

**4. Claim what you haven't claimed.**
Unclaimed listings are the most common source of outdated info, because
nobody's watching them. Claiming a listing usually just takes verifying you
own the business — a quick process on most platforms.

**5. Fix it once, then check twice a year.**
Once everything matches, put a reminder on your calendar for every six
months. Any time you move, change your number, or update your hours, that's
also your cue to update every platform, not just Google.

## The takeaway

This isn't glamorous work, and it won't make for an exciting social post. But
it's the kind of unglamorous fix that removes friction between your business
and customers who are already looking for you — and it's the sort of thing
search engines quietly reward. An hour of cleanup now can mean fewer confused
customers and a small, steady bump in how easily people find you.
